dd-goldfish — Doomsday goldfish simulator ⓘ methodology

ⓘ How this works & known caveats

What it is. A Monte-Carlo simulation: thousands of real games are played out by a rules-accurate MTG engine (stack, priority, mana, CR-correct resolution — spells resolve while on the stack, searches are real choices, etc.), with a pluggable "goldfishing" strategy as the pilot. "Goldfishing" means solitaire — no opponent: we measure only how fast the deck assembles its combo, never whether it would win a real game.

The objective. Everything is scored by P(cast Doomsday by the cut-off turn). The cut-off is the turn past which a slow combo is assumed dead (raced / Wastelanded). A slow hand is therefore nearly worthless, so the pilot plays for speed.

How the pilot decides (gameplay). A backward solver estimates, for any state, P(cast by cut-off) (g) from a hypergeometric model: deterministic mana assembly, plus drawing into the missing piece, with cantrips counted as extra card "looks." If a guaranteed (no-draw) line lands by the cut-off it settles and executes it; otherwise it gambles — develops a black source and digs (and rips a fetch when even the best case is too slow).

How the pilot mulligans. Selectable, because the headline rate shifts a lot with how hard you ship hands: Realistic (default) ignores "air" cards (Wasteland, LED, Thassa, …) and keeps a hand with real mana and a concrete plan: a guaranteed line; Doomsday + a black source + a dig; a deterministic Tamiyo flip by turn 2; or, unflooded, two looks (or one look + a supporter). It ships no-mana / flooded / do-nothing hands — an explicit, inspectable rule that does not consult g. Keep 7 never mulligans — the deck's raw speed, with no mulligan decisions mixed in.

Structural caveats (true for any deck).

  • The true P(ttd ≤ K) is genuinely hard to compute. The exact probability that the combo lands by turn K depends on library order, chained card selection (a cantrip that finds another cantrip), and play sequencing — it is intractable to evaluate exactly. g is a deliberately tractable approximation, so it carries bias. Everything below follows from that.
  • g is pessimistic. It ranks hands correctly (monotone) but tends to under-predict the true rate, worst on weak hands (the mana model is single-resource; the cantrip terms are approximate).
  • Cantrip "looks" are a heuristic. Non-additive and time-capped, with no true modeling of chained cantrips.
  • Mulligan modes change the result. The headline rate is reported for the selected mode; comparing a deck across both modes (Keep 7 → Realistic) is the honest read. Neither consults g — Realistic is a pure card-shape rule, Keep 7 never mulligans.
  • The per-hand predicted P is the model's own estimate of g; the headline % is the measured simulation rate — the honest number, free of that bias.
How this mode decides
The cut-off and the solver

Cut-off turn. The deadline for the combo. A send after it is treated as too slow to matter, since by then the opponent has raced you or destroyed your mana. Every result is scored as P(send by the cut-off) — resolve Doomsday or pop the Fantasticar — so a lower cut-off rewards faster decks.

How P(send) is computed — a hypergeometric approximation. For any game state the solver reasons backward from the payoff. If a guaranteed line lands by the cut-off with no draws, P(send) = 1. Otherwise it estimates: for each missing piece (a black source, the payoff, the car's fuel) it counts the matching cards still in the library (the “outs”) and takes the hypergeometric chance of drawing enough of them by the cut-off — drawing without replacement — where each castable cantrip adds to the draw budget (the extra cards it sees). The pieces are combined by multiplying their marginal probabilities, and the two win-conditions as P(send) = 1 − (1−PDD)(1−Pcar). It's deliberately an approximation — it assumes the pieces (and the two win-cons) are independent and treats cantrips as blind extra draws rather than modelling chained selection exactly — tractable, and good enough to rank hands and guide play (it runs a guaranteed line when one finishes by the cut-off, else develops mana and digs).

How the pilot digs (cantrips)

Every card-selection decision falls out of one number: P(send by the cut-off), re-derived from the current game state. The pilot never uses a card value table — it picks the option that leaves that probability highest.

  • Ponder reveals three. It compares the best arrangement of those three on top (draw them over the next turns) against shuffling them away and keeps whichever scores higher — so a brick top gets shuffled, a live top gets kept.
  • Brainstorm draws three, then puts back the two cards the line needs least, keeping payoffs, mana, and fuel.
  • Consider / surveil keeps the revealed card on top only if it scores at least as high as a fresh unknown draw; otherwise it bins it.
  • Scry / Preordain keeps the arrangement (rest to the bottom) that scores highest.
  • Fetch / tutor / dig pick the target that most raises the probability — a dual that pays the next pip, or staging the payoff itself on top.
