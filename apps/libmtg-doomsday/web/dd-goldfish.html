dd-goldfish — Doomsday goldfish simulator ⓘ methodology

ⓘ How this works & known caveats

What it is. A Monte-Carlo simulation: thousands of real games are played out by a rules-accurate MTG engine (stack, priority, mana, CR-correct resolution — spells resolve while on the stack, searches are real choices, etc.), with a pluggable "goldfishing" strategy as the pilot. "Goldfishing" means solitaire — no opponent: we measure only how fast the deck assembles its combo, never whether it would win a real game.

The objective. Everything is scored by P(cast Doomsday by the cut-off turn). The cut-off is the turn past which a slow combo is assumed dead (raced / Wastelanded). A slow hand is therefore nearly worthless, so the pilot plays for speed.

How the pilot decides (gameplay). A backward solver estimates, for any state, P(cast by cut-off) (g) from a hypergeometric model: deterministic mana assembly, plus drawing into the missing piece, with cantrips counted as extra card "looks." If a guaranteed (no-draw) line lands by the cut-off it settles and executes it; otherwise it gambles — develops a black source and digs (and rips a fetch when even the best case is too slow).

How the pilot mulligans. Selectable, because the headline rate shifts a lot with how hard you ship hands: Realistic (default) ignores "air" cards (Wasteland, LED, Thassa, …) and keeps a hand with real mana and a concrete plan: a guaranteed line; Doomsday + a black source + a dig; a deterministic Tamiyo flip by turn 2; or, unflooded, two looks (or one look + a supporter). It ships no-mana / flooded / do-nothing hands — an explicit, inspectable rule that does not consult g. Keep 7 never mulligans (raw deck speed). Aggressive re-draws any hand below a high g threshold (0.55 / 0.38 / 0.20 by mulligan) — fast but it ships almost every imperfect seven and so flattens deck differences.

Structural caveats (true for any deck).

  • The true P(ttd ≤ K) is genuinely hard to compute. The exact probability that the combo lands by turn K depends on library order, chained card selection (a cantrip that finds another cantrip), and play sequencing — it is intractable to evaluate exactly. g is a deliberately tractable approximation, so it carries bias. Everything below follows from that.
  • g is pessimistic. It ranks hands correctly (monotone) but tends to under-predict the true rate, worst on weak hands (the mana model is single-resource; the cantrip terms are approximate).
  • Cantrip "looks" are a heuristic. Non-additive and time-capped, with no true modeling of chained cantrips.
  • Mulligan modes change the result. The headline rate is reported for the selected mode; comparing a deck across modes (Keep 7 → Realistic → Aggressive) is the honest read. Only the Aggressive mode consults g; Realistic is a pure card-shape rule.
  • The per-hand predicted P is the model's own estimate of g; the headline % is the measured simulation rate — the honest number, free of that bias.
How this mode decides
The cut-off and the solver

Cut-off turn. The deadline for the combo. A Doomsday cast after it is treated as too slow to matter, since by then the opponent has raced you or destroyed your mana. Every result is scored as P(cast Doomsday by the cut-off), so a lower cut-off rewards faster decks.

Backwards solver. For any game state, it reasons backward from Doomsday to estimate that probability: the mana it can assemble for certain, plus the chance of drawing a missing piece, counting each cantrip as a few extra cards seen. The same estimate guides play — it runs a guaranteed line when one finishes by the cut-off, and otherwise develops a black source and digs toward the combo.
